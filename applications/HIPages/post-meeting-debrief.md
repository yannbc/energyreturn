# hipages / post-meeting debrief

Conversation with Jeremy Burton, CTO, on 21 April 2026. First meeting. Roughly an hour. Initial conversation off the back of a LinkedIn outreach, not a formal interview process.

## What he revealed

The conversation went deeper than expected. He shared organisational dissatisfaction, R&D ratio framing, M&A appetite, and engineering culture concerns. He didn't have to share any of those. The fact that he did is the strongest signal from the meeting.

**On what he wants in a product leader (without throwing Herry under the bus).** He wants more experimentation, more stealth-released features, less analysis paralysis. The current org is too data-driven before deploying. He wants product leadership that pushes the org to ship rougher and learn faster. He admitted directly that they have "underrated the opportunity cost of building things perfectly." That is a CTO confessing, not complaining.

**On engineering culture.** He wants engineers who own outcomes, not engineers who execute requirements. Current team is too much in order-taker mode. He wants more engineers in the problem space, working alongside product on what's worth building, not just on how to build it. This is a deeper culture problem than any single hire fixes, but it shapes the kind of product partnership he actually wants.

**On domain structure.** He has walked back his year-old podcast position on organising around domains. He now thinks AI has changed the calculus and people should span domains because the most valuable problems span domains. This implies an org redesign at some point.

**On the data analyst role.** Being obviated. SQL-as-a-job is dying because product people and engineers can do it themselves now. He's not afraid to obsolete roles when the technology moves underneath them. Tells you he'd back a product leader making hard structural calls.

**On the competitive position in job management.** They don't need to feature-match the best job management tools. They just need to "do enough." Job management exists to defend the bundle, not to win the standalone job management market. This is a strategic posture, not a copout. Significantly sharper than I had it framed in the prep.

**On investor expectations.** R&D-as-percentage-of-revenue needs to come down. Not by cutting R&D but by growing revenue faster than R&D headcount. Story they need to tell investors is "we're shipping more with the same team, AI is making us more efficient." That maps directly onto the Claude Code adoption and the cross-domain push.

**On M&A and partnerships.** Inorganic growth is on the table to satisfy investor growth expectations. He couldn't disclose specifics. Implies the platform thesis is real and active, not slideware. A senior product role would likely be part of corporate development conversations.

**On his own ambitions.** Mostly market sensitive. He couldn't say much. The fact that he flagged it as market sensitive rather than vague tells me there's something concrete he's not sharing.

**Homework he set.** He's sending a link to their product vision video. Watch it within 24 hours of receiving. Send a specific reaction within 48 hours. Not a glowing reaction. A sharp one.

## What I observed that he didn't articulate

**He doesn't know the median tradie's share-of-wallet from hipages.** This is the foundational metric for the bundle thesis. If the median tradie gets 60% of jobs from hipages, the bundle is sticky. If they get 15%, the bundle is fragile and a meaningful chunk of the subscription base is paying for job management at multiple times the standalone price, with churn risk to ServiceM8 and Tradify.

Three possible explanations for him not knowing:
1. He knows but couldn't say (market sensitive). Unlikely given how forthcoming he was on other commercially sensitive things.
2. The data is messy. Plausible. Job management adoption is only at 15% (page 16), so the dataset to triangulate share-of-wallet only exists for the Power User cohort, which is a biased sample.
3. Nobody's been routinely asking the question. The one that should worry him on his own behalf. If the strategic foundation of the bundle is "tradies stay because hipages is their lead engine," and the executive team isn't tracking the metric that proves or disproves that, then the bundle is operating on faith.

This is a "next conversation" question, not a follow-up email line. Sharper to deliver in person where it has the weight of a deliberately considered question rather than a passing observation.

**The energy-into-the-home parallel.** Their page 22 expansion ambitions (payments, finance, insurance, procurement, reputation management, householder monetisation) replicate the pattern Billcap and DC Power Co both ran into. Data-stream stickiness does not automatically convert to relationship stickiness. The customer trusts you with the boring transactional data but doesn't transfer that trust to the higher-value adjacent product.

Wrinkle: the analogy isn't perfect. Energy customers are passive. Hipages tradies are active small business owners already paying $215/month, so the trust threshold for adjacent products may be lower. But the failure modes are identical, and most of his potential product hires won't have lived experience of this pattern. This is the most valuable thing I bring to the next conversation.

Hold this one for a second meeting. Too rich for the follow-up email. It's a 20-minute conversation in its own right with the specific Billcap and DC Power Co examples.

## How my read of the opportunity changed

Going in: peer conversation that might lead to a role.

Coming out: organisational design problem he's trying to solve, with me as one possible part of the answer. That's a co-design framing, not an interview process. Same shape as the James Eddison conversation at Octopus.

The role is not a Herry replacement. It's the antidote to what wasn't working with Herry. That is a much better fit for my background than the SVP Product job spec would suggest.

## Strategic reframes to carry forward

**The H2 features are defensive, not offensive.** Voice in Smart Quotes, Location, Route, Follow Up Questions exist to keep tradies inside the bundle, not to win the job management market. This changes the strategic question. The right question for next time is: "If job management is defensive and lead generation is offensive, where does the next dollar of investment go? How do you balance the build between the two?"

**The bundle thesis depends on a number Jeremy doesn't have.** Median tradie share-of-wallet from hipages. The whole defensive-bundle logic only works if the lead engine is structurally dominant in tradies' job flow. If it's not, the $215/month is fragile.

**The platform expansion (Proptech Labs, Builderscrack, page 22 services) is the energy-into-the-home pattern.** Trust transfer is harder than data ownership suggests. I have lived this. Most of his options don't.

**The investor narrative is shifting from growth to efficient growth.** R&D as a percentage of revenue down, revenue per engineer up. Productivity gains from AI are the public story. The product leader who lands that narrative for him is valuable beyond the role itself.

## Next moves

**Today.** Send the three-line follow-up email. Reference the build-perfect comment specifically. Offer the Pendula V2-to-V3 mechanics. Do not ask for next steps.

**Within 48 hours of receiving the product vision video.** Watch it. Send a specific, sharp reaction. One observation. One implication that follows. One curious question. That's it.

**For the next conversation if it happens.** Lead with the share-of-wallet question. Have the energy-into-the-home parallel ready as a second-act conversation. Be ready to talk about the defensive-versus-offensive investment balance question.

**Carry forward to any senior product role conversation.** The way this conversation went confirms a few things about how I should frame myself with CTOs more broadly. The peer-conversation opener works. Asking what's actually hard before pitching anything works. Naming the strategic posture (engagement curve, defensive bundle, etc) in the language the finance team would use works. Reframing the role from "candidate slot" to "organisational problem to co-design" works. These are repeatable patterns.

## The single sentence that lands now

The killer sentence from the prep ("AI on rails, not AI end to end, and your matching engine is the rails") is no longer the right one because Jeremy has now told me job management is defensive. The new version, for the next conversation:

> "Your bundle is defensive on job management and offensive on lead generation. The page 22 expansion is the third leg, but it only works if tradies trust you enough to buy adjacent products from you. That's the bet I'd want to be near."

Drop once. Do not repeat.
